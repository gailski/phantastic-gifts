About the project

On Phantastic Gifts

A digital project on Louis Lewin — and the archive that remembers him unevenly.

The Project

Phantastic Gifts is a digital research project on the life, correspondence, and scientific legacy of Louis Lewin (1850–1929): German-Jewish pharmacologist, toxicologist, ethnobotanist, and one of modernity's earliest systematic readers of intoxication. It functions as a companion to an in-progress doctoral dissertation and as a standing archive in its own right.

The site gathers four related streams of research: an interpretive exhibition built from archival objects; a searchable directory of Lewin's correspondents; a map of his Berlin; and a world map of his international network. Each builds on work conducted in the Nachlass Louis Lewin at the Staatsbibliothek zu Berlin and supplementary collections in Germany and abroad.

The Author

Abigail Trozenski is a doctoral candidate at Vanderbilt University, where she is completing a dissertation on Louis Lewin and the formation of modern toxicology.

Principal Archive

The core source is the Nachlass Louis Lewin held at the Staatsbibliothek zu Berlin – Preußischer Kulturbesitz (SBB-PK). The collection includes Lewin's incoming correspondence, manuscripts, photographs, and family papers. His outgoing correspondence does not survive as a collection, which shapes both the methodological approach of this project and the interpretive work of reading against archival silence.

Additional materials consulted include the Beringer family archive; the Pressemappe 20. Jahrhundert (PM20) of the Hamburgisches Welt-Wirtschafts-Archiv, now held by the ZBW – Leibniz-Informationszentrum Wirtschaft in Hamburg and Kiel; and records at the Jüdischer Friedhof Weißensee.

Method

The project reads Lewin's archive as what Jacques Derrida called a pharmakon: a body of knowledge that is simultaneously remedy and poison, preservation and corruption. Lewin's Nachlass survives through decades of family curation, institutional transfer, Nazi-era destruction and calibrated sparing, wartime loss, and postwar reassembly. The silences in the archive are themselves historical evidence — and reading them as such is one of the project's central methodological commitments.

A Pharmaceutical Timeline
Substances, Laws, and the World Lewin Moved Through

From Sertürner’s isolation of morphine in 1805 to the United Nations Convention on Psychotropic Substances in 1971, this timeline traces the development, commercialization, and regulation of the substances central to Louis Lewin’s toxicological and pharmacological work. Entries marked in gold indicate Lewin’s direct involvement; entries marked in red are milestones of drug legislation.

Lewin’s Involvement
1805 – 1859

Alkaloid Chemistry and the Birth of Modern Pharmacy

1805
Friedrich Sertürner isolates morphine from opium in Paderborn, launching the science of alkaloid chemistry.
Friedrich Sertürner (1783–1841)
1819
Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge isolates caffeine from coffee.
Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge (1794–1867)
1827
Merck, founded as the Engel-Apotheke in Darmstadt, begins commercial manufacture of morphine — the first industrial-scale alkaloid production.
1831
Chloroform synthesized independently by Justus von Liebig, Eugène Soubeiran, and Samuel Guthrie.
Justus von Liebig (1803–1873)
1832
Justus von Liebig discovers chloral hydrate at Gießen; its sedative potential will be recognized decades later.
1847
Rudolf Buchheim founds the world’s first pharmacology department at the University of Dorpat, establishing pharmacology as an independent experimental discipline.
Rudolf Buchheim (1820–1879)
1848
Paraldehyde synthesized by Valentin Weidenbusch in Liebig’s laboratory; later adopted as a hypnotic and sedative.
1853–55
The hypodermic syringe is developed independently by Alexander Wood in Edinburgh and Charles Pravaz in Lyon. Morphine use expands dramatically in medical practice.
1856
Friedrich Wöhler asks Austrian explorer Karl Ritter von Scherzer to bring coca leaves from South America aboard the frigate Novara during its circumnavigation.
Friedrich Wöhler (1800–1882) · Karl Ritter von Scherzer (1821–1903)
1859
Wöhler receives the coca shipment and passes it to his Göttingen student Albert Niemann, who isolates the principal alkaloid and names it cocaine.
Albert Niemann (1834–1861)
1861 – 1897

Regulation, Ethnobotany, and the Rise of Industrial Pharmacy

1861
Rudolf Buchheim demonstrates the sedative properties of chloral hydrate, opening the modern hypnotic era.
1868
The United Kingdom passes the Pharmacy Act — the first modern legislation restricting the sale of opium and other poisons to licensed pharmacists.
1869
Oscar Liebreich publishes on the sedative and hypnotic properties of chloral hydrate, popularizing its clinical use.
Oscar Liebreich (1839–1908)
1871
The Imperial Health Office (Kaiserliches Gesundheitsamt) is established in Berlin, centralizing public-health regulation in the newly unified German Empire.
1874
C. R. Alder Wright synthesizes diacetylmorphine — later branded as heroin — by boiling morphine with acetic anhydride at St. Mary’s Hospital, London. His discovery receives little attention at the time.
Charles Romley Alder Wright (1844–1894)
1877
German chemical industry organizes politically with the founding of the Verein zur Wahrung der Interessen der chemischen Industrie Deutschlands.
1880
Albert Ladenburg isolates scopolamine (hyoscine), later combined with morphine in surgical anesthesia from 1900 onward.
Albert Ladenburg (1842–1911)
1887
Louis Lewin receives peyote — “muscale buttons” — from the Dallas physician J. R. Briggs, distributed via Parke-Davis during his five-month North American journey. Company records note: “Muscale Button given to L. Lewin, Berlin, for investigation.”
Louis Lewin (1850–1929) · John Raleigh Briggs (1851–1907)
1887
Romanian chemist Lazăr Edeleanu synthesizes amphetamine (phenylisopropylamine) at the University of Berlin; its pharmacological potential will go unrecognized for five decades.
Lazăr Edeleanu (1861–1941)
1888
Sulfonal synthesized by Eugen Baumann; Alfred Kast introduces it into clinical practice as a hypnotic.
1888
At the Royal Botanical Museum in Berlin, Paul Hennings formally describes the peyote cactus as Anhalonium lewinii, naming the species in Lewin’s honor.
Paul Hennings (1841–1908)
1894
Lewin publishes Die Pfeilgifte, his landmark study of arrow poisons. The same year, Merck crystallizes anhalonine from Lewin’s peyote material — the first pure alkaloid isolated from the Cactaceae family.
1897
Arthur Heffter isolates mescaline from peyote, identifying it as the principal psychoactive alkaloid in Anhalonium lewinii.
Arthur Heffter (1859–1925)
1898 – 1918

Pharmaceutical Modernity and the Expansion of Regulation

1898
Bayer markets heroin (diamorphine), re-synthesized by Felix Hoffmann, as a cough suppressant and “non-addictive” morphine substitute. Lewin would become a vocal early critic of the drug’s unregulated circulation.
1900
The Reichsgesundheitsrat is created alongside the Reichsseuchengesetz, consolidating German public-health advisory structures.
1902
Emil Fischer — one of Lewin’s scientific correspondents — and Joseph von Mering synthesize Veronal (barbital), the first commercially successful barbiturate.
Emil Fischer (1852–1919) · Joseph von Mering (1849–1908)
1904
Bayer commercializes Veronal, inaugurating the barbiturate era of sedatives and hypnotics.
1907
Knoll AG patents bromural (bromisoval), a mild sedative widely prescribed through the 1930s.
1909
The United States passes the Smoking Opium Exclusion Act, criminalizing non-medical opium importation — the beginning of the twentieth-century American war on drugs.
1912
Merck chemist Anton Köllisch synthesizes MDMA as an intermediate compound in the preparation of hydrastinine; the firm obtains a patent in 1913. MDMA’s psychoactive properties will not be investigated for decades.
Anton Köllisch (1888–1916)
1912
The International Opium Convention at The Hague establishes the first multilateral treaty framework on narcotic drugs.
1914
The United States Harrison Narcotics Tax Act places cocaine and opiates under federal registration and taxation, effectively criminalizing non-medical use.
1916
Oxycodone synthesized from thebaine at the University of Frankfurt by Martin Freund and Edmund Speyer.
Martin Freund (1863–1920) · Edmund Speyer (1878–1942)
1917
Merck introduces oxycodone into clinical use under the trade name Eukodal, marketed as both an analgesic and a cough suppressant.
1918
Arthur Stoll isolates ergotamine from ergot fungus at Sandoz in Basel, laying the chemical groundwork for later ergoline research.
Arthur Stoll (1887–1971)
1918
The Kaiserliches Gesundheitsamt is renamed the Reichsgesundheitsamt following the collapse of the Empire.
1919 – 1929

Phantastica, Prohibition, and Late-Weimar Drug Control

1919
Ernst Späth synthesizes mescaline, the first laboratory synthesis of a psychedelic alkaloid. It marks mescaline’s entry into the psychiatric research agenda.
Ernst Späth (1886–1946)
1919–32
Finland enforces national prohibition of alcohol (kieltolaki).
1920
Lewin publishes Die Gifte in der Weltgeschichte and Die Kohlenoxydvergiftung with Julius Springer — the beginning of his major postwar toxicological synthesis.
1920–33
The Eighteenth Amendment enacts national alcohol prohibition in the United States.
1924
Lewin publishes Phantastica: Die betäubenden und erregenden Genußmittel with Georg Stilke, introducing his five-group taxonomy of intoxicants: Euphorica, Phantastica, Inebriantia, Hypnotica, Excitantia.
1927
Kurt Beringer — Lewin’s correspondent on visionary drugs — publishes Der Meskalinrausch, the standard German-language monograph on mescaline experience.
Kurt Beringer (1893–1949)
1928
Lewin publishes his research on Banisteria caapi, identifying its active principle as banisterine. Merck subsequently shows banisterine is identical to harmine and devotes the first nineteen pages of its 1928 Jahresbericht to the compound’s therapeutic prospects.
1928
Merck introduces SEE (renamed Scophedal in 1942), a combination analgesic of scopolamine, oxycodone (Eukodal), and ephedrine.
1929
Lewin and the Berlin jurist Wenzel Goldbaum co-author a commentary on the German Opiumgesetz — the country’s first comprehensive drug-control law — in its final legislative phase. Lewin dies in Berlin on December 1.
Wenzel Goldbaum (1881–1970)
1933 – 1971

Postwar Rediscovery and the International Control Regime

1933
Working at Sandoz in Basel on ergoline derivatives, Albert Hofmann first synthesizes LSD-25; its psychoactive properties are accidentally discovered in 1943.
Albert Hofmann (1906–2008)
1952
The Bundesgesundheitsamt (BGA) is founded as the successor to the Reichsgesundheitsamt, resuming centralized public-health oversight in the Federal Republic.
1961
The United Nations Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs consolidates earlier treaties into a unified international control regime covering opiates and cocaine.
1967
LSD is made illegal in the United States under California state law (followed by federal scheduling in 1968).
1971
The UN Convention on Psychotropic Substances extends international control to synthetic drugs including amphetamines and LSD. Germany passes the Betäubungsmittelgesetz (BtMG), its modern narcotics law, the same year.

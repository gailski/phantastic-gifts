Introduction · Einleitung

Louis Lewin was born in 1850 in Tuchel, West Prussia, into a Jewish family navigating the precarious terrain of assimilation and identity in the German Empire. He would become the founder of modern toxicology, a pioneer in psychopharmacology, and the author of works that shaped how the twentieth century understood the relationship between human beings and intoxicating substances.

The title of this exhibition plays on the German word Gift, which means poison — a false friend to its English cognate meaning present or offering. This linguistic duality mirrors Lewin's central insight: every substance is both remedy and danger, and the boundary between the two is measured in dosage.

This exhibition brings together portraits, poetry, botanical illustrations, passenger manifests, letters, and the taxonomic architecture of his masterwork Phantastica to reconstruct the world of a scientist whose legacy was deliberately obscured by the regime that consumed his homeland.

“Alle Dinge sind Gift, und nichts ist ohne Gift; allein die Dosis macht, daß ein Ding kein Gift ist.”
— Paracelsus (1493–1541)

Pharmakon · φάρμακον

The Word That Means Both

In ancient Greek, pharmakon (φαρμακον) designated simultaneously the remedy and the poison, the cure and the curse. Jacques Derrida's reading of Plato's Phaedrus revealed how this irreducible ambiguity structured Western thought about writing, medicine, and knowledge itself. For Lewin, this was not philosophy but laboratory fact: a substance relieves suffering at one dose and produces injury at another. He argued toxicity was never about the substance alone — it depended on dose, exposure, environment, and the social distribution of harm.

Gift (English)

A present, an offering, something freely given. From Proto-Germanic *giftiz: that which is given.

Gift (Deutsch)

Poison. A substance that destroys. Same root, inverted meaning — the shadow twin of generosity.

A Life in Science · Lebenslauf

From Tuchel to Berlin and the world

9 November 1850

Born in Tuchel, West Prussia

A West Prussian borderland — German, Polish, Jewish, contested. His parents Hirsch and Rahel Appelbaum were Orthodox Jews in a predominantly Christian town.

1856

Migration to Berlin

The family settled on Grenadierstraße in the Scheunenviertel, Berlin’s most stigmatized Jewish quarter. He visited his parents there every day of his life for the rest of theirs.

1864–1875

Gymnasium and Doctorate

Admitted through his mother Rahel’s advocacy. Mentored by the orientalist Paul de Lagarde — later a vocal antisemite — then earned his doctorate at Friedrich-Wilhelms-Universität in 1875 and habilitated in pharmacology, toxicology, and hygiene in 1881.

1883

Marries Clara Bernhardine Wolff

Clara came from a teacher’s family in Osnabrück. She became his lifelong companion, the addressee of his travel diary, and the recipient of his letters from across the world.

1886

Pioneering Mescaline Research

Among the first European scientists to conduct laboratory studies of peyote (Anhalonium Lewinii, named in his honor), analyzing its alkaloids and effects on human consciousness.

1887

Journey to North America

Embarks on a five-month research journey across the United States and Canada, studying indigenous uses of psychoactive plants, visiting laboratories, and encountering the infrastructures of industrial modernity.

1893–1920s

Institutional Exclusion

Despite an extraordinary publication record, Lewin was repeatedly denied a full professorship. He maintained a private laboratory in his Berlin apartment — an act of persistence against institutional antisemitism.

1924

Phantastica Published

Publishes his magnum opus, Phantastica: Die betäubenden und erregenden Genussmittel, the first systematic classification of psychoactive substances. A foundational text in the genealogy of psychedelic science.

1 December 1929

Death in Berlin

He died at 79. Clara survived him by fourteen years, only to be murdered in Theresienstadt in January 1943. Their daughter Gertrud was murdered there too. Their remains are lost.

Poetry · Dichtung

Lewin the Poet

Verses from the travel diary, written for Clara across the Atlantic

Aus dem Reisetagebuch · From the travel diary
Ausreise
Written aboard the SS Hammonia, Atlantic Ocean · 31 July 1887
Deutsch
Sonnenglanz liegt auf dem Meere Dessen Fläche, leichtbewegt Von des Westwinds Flügelschlage, Mich geduldig zielwärts trägt. Abseits von dem Menschenstrome, An die Schiffswand angelehnt, Denke ich, erfüllt von Liebe Derer, die mein Herz ersehnt. Denke an die lieben Kleinen Und mein Weib, die weit zurück Meergetrennt von mir nun leben — Fern dem Blick, mein fernes Glück! Meine Lippen sprechen leise Was mein ganzes Ich bewegt, Senden Worte auf zu Jenem, Der das Menschenschicksal trägt: Halt, o Gott mir meine Lieben Immerfort in guter Hut, Niemals lasse sie entbehren Der Gesundheit höchstes Gut! Schütze Du auch meine Pfade Leite mich zum Heimatherd, Daß ich gesund ich wiederfinde Alle, die mein Herz begehrt!
English · Translation by the author
Sunlight lies upon the sea Whose surface, gently moved By the wing-beat of the west wind, Carries me patiently toward my goal. Apart from the stream of people, Leaning against the ship’s wall, I think, filled with love Of those whom my heart longs for. I think of the dear little ones And my wife, who now lives far behind, Separated from me by the sea — Far from sight, my distant happiness! My lips speak softly What moves my whole being, Sending words upward to Him Who carries the fate of mankind: Hold, O God, my loved ones Forever in good keeping, Never let them be deprived Of health, the highest good! Protect also my own path, Lead me to my home hearth, That I may find again in health All those whom my heart desires!
Written after the train derailment at Kamloops
25 August 1887 · Somewhere in British Columbia
Deutsch
Mein theures Weib, ihr süßen Kinder, An wen als Euch hab' ich gedacht, Als jähes Unglück mich bedrohte In der vergangenen, finstern Nacht! Ihr wart die Engel, die mich schützten Und Euer Beten war mein Hort — „Den Vater gieb gesund uns wieder“! So tönt zu Gott auf dieses Wort. Erhörung fand der Liebe Flehen, Das Fallen aus dem Kindermund — Für sie, die meiner noch bedürfen Erhielt der Höchste mich gesund.
English · Translation by the author
My dear wife, my sweet children, Who else but you did I think of, When sudden misfortune threatened me In the dark terror of that night! You were the angels who protected me And your prayer was my refuge — “Give us back our father, healthy!” So it rose to God on this word. The love’s plea found its answer, The words falling from children’s mouths — For them, who still need me, The Almighty kept me well.

After a train derailment on the Canadian Pacific Railway near Kamloops, British Columbia, Lewin tended to injured passengers through the night. He wrote this poem for Clara in the hours after.

Aus dem Reisetagebuch · From the travel diary
Heimkehr
Written aboard the SS Wieland, returning to Hamburg · September 1887
Deutsch
Zur Heimath! Welch' wonniger Klang! Nie ahnte ich, was die Heimath ist! Jetzt weiß ich's, wo ich so lang Kinder, Weib und heimische Sitte vermißt. Großes sah ich in Ost und West — Gewaltige Ströme, üppigsten Wald Himmelstrebende Felsen mit dem Adlernest Und Flächen schier endlos in Gestalt! Staunen ergriff mich allüberall Sinn und Geist war gefangen — Doch das Herz im nachtönenden Wiederhall Nach der Heimath trägt es Verlangen Sei ruhig Meer, seid günstig Winde Führt schnelle mich den langen Pfad, Daß in der Heimath die Lieben ich finde Und auf die Ruhe folget die That.
English · Translation by the author
Home! What a blissful sound! I never knew what home was! Now I know it, having so long missed Children, wife, and familiar ways. Great things I saw in east and west — Mighty rivers, the richest forest, Sky-reaching cliffs with the eagle’s nest And plains seemingly endless in form! Wonder seized me everywhere, Sense and spirit were captive — But the heart, in its echoing resonance, Longs after home Be calm, sea, be favorable, winds, Lead me quickly the long path, That in home I find my loved ones And after rest follows action.

The 1887 Journey · Reise

Modernity in Motion

In July 1887, Louis Lewin boarded the SS Hammonia in Hamburg and crossed the Atlantic with his uncle. Over five months he traveled from New York to Niagara Falls, across Canada by rail on the Canadian Pacific Railway, down to San Francisco, back east through Denver and Kansas City, to Washington for the U.S. Medical Congress, through Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Detroit, Chicago, and Boston, before returning from New York aboard the SS Wieland on 29 September 1887.

Hamburg → Atlantic

New York

11–15 August

Central Park, Metropolitan Museum, the Battery, Staten Island. Waited anxiously for Clara's letter.

Niagara Falls

17 August

Visited the Whirlpool, Goat Island, and the Suspension Bridge. Called it a triumph of human calculation.

Montreal

18–19 August

Visited McGill College and Mount Royal Park. Boarded the Canadian Pacific Railway.

Canadian Rockies

20–25 August

Rail through Winnipeg, Regina, Medicine Hat. Survived a train derailment at Kamloops.

San Francisco

28–30 August

Palace Hotel. Explored Chinatown, visited opium dens, sketched the floor plan from memory.

Washington, D.C.

4–7 September

U.S. Medical Congress. Visited the Smithsonian Institution. Explored Pennsylvania Avenue.

Return Voyage

29 September

Boarded the SS Wieland from New York. Wrote the Heimkehr poem at sea.

Phantastica

The Five Kingdoms of Intoxication

In 1924, Lewin published Phantastica, proposing the first rigorous taxonomy of psychoactive substances. He organized them not by chemical structure but by their effects on human consciousness — a phenomenological classification that anticipated modern psychopharmacology by decades.

Euphorica

Sedatives of Mental Activity

Substances that diminish or suspend mental activity and reduce pain and distress.

Opium · Morphine · Heroin · Cocaine

Phantastica

Agents of Hallucination

Substances that produce illusions, hallucinations, and altered states of perception.

Peyote · Cannabis · Fly Agaric · Ayahuasca

Inebriantia

Agents of Intoxication

Substances producing initial excitation followed by depression of mental function.

Alcohol · Ether · Chloroform · Benzene

Hypnotica

Agents of Sleep

Substances that mechanically induce sleep by depressing cerebral activity.

Chloral hydrate · Barbiturates · Bromides

Excitantia

Agents of Stimulation

Substances that stimulate mental activity without subsequent depression.

Coffee · Tea · Tobacco · Betel · Khat

Correspondence · Korrespondenz

Epilogue · Epilog

In Memoriam

He classified the substances that alter consciousness, but could not have classified the madness that consumed his country four years after his death. His name was struck from the record; his work attributed to others or simply forgotten. This exhibition is an act of restoration.

This digital exhibition was created as part of doctoral research at Vanderbilt University. It draws on Lewin’s surviving Nachlass at the Staatsbibliothek zu Berlin–Preußischer Kulturbesitz, the private Lewin-Sachs family archive, and the posthumously published travel diary Durch die USA und Canada im Jahre 1887, ed. Bo Holmstedt and Karlheinz Lohs (Akademie-Verlag Berlin, 1985).

Photographs of the Weißensee grave were taken by the author during research visits in summer 2022. All diary translations from the German are by the author unless otherwise noted.
